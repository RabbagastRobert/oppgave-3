The most distant objects made on Earth

Credit: Kim Shiflett

During the last 80 years or so, mankind has sent many probes to learn more about the planets in our solar system. Some of witch are travelling so fast that they are able to escape the gravity of our sun and begin their interstellar journeys.

In this article we will have a look at the 5 most distant of these probes and how far they've gone.

New Horizons

Launched January 19th, 2006, New Horizons is the 5th most distant object launched from Earth. It's primary mission was to study Pluto and at least one more Kuiper belt object.

New Horizons was launched on an Atlas V rocket, and at the time it was the fastest object launched from Earth with a speed measuring 58.500 km/h. (a record that has been vastly beaten by the Parker Solar Probe in 2023)

After a flyby and a gravity assist by Jupiter in 2007, New Horizons set the course for Pluto where it arrived in 2015. By then it was travelling at a stunning speed of 84.000 km/h at a distance of 12.500 km. It took the most detailed images of Pluto to this day.

The probe is now flying through the Kuiper belt, and is expected to exit the belt in 2028 / 2029. Unfortunatly, New Horizons, is planned to be shut down before then because of budget cuts at NASA.

New Horizons is as of September 2025, 9.2 billion km from Earth. That is the same as 62.2 AU. It has no final destination and is believed to orbit the galaxy forever. Right now it is heading in the direction of the Saggitarius constellation.

1 AU (Astronomical Unit) = 149.597.871 km = 8.3 light minutes = about the average distance between the Sun and Earth, however this has changed to be exactly 149.597.870.700 m as of 2012.

Pioneer 11

Launched on an Atlas-Centaur rocket on april 6th, 1973, Pioneer 11 is the 4th most distant object to leave the Solar system. Its primary mission was to study Jupiter and Saturn, and also the solar wind and cosmic rays.

Pioneer 11 started its flyby of Jupiter in december 1975 where it took detailed images of Jupiters Red Spot. It also gathered data and images of several of Jupiters moons; Callisto, Ganymede, Io, Europa and Amalthea.

In late august 1979 it arrived at the Saturnian system. While there, it took pictures of Saturns rings while actually travelling through the rings. This was done to ensure that Voyager 2 could travel the same route through the rings without taking damage. Pioneer 11 was a true pioneer in that sense. This was done because the scientists thought it would be better to know ahead of time if it actually could be done. By doing this, two more moons was discovered sharing the same orbit. The probe nearly collided with one of them, travelling at a distance of only 4.000 km at the closest approach. Later, Pioneer 11 flew by Janus at a distance of only 2.500 km, however this was a planned encounter.

Pioneer 11 had flybys of 13 out of the 274 moons of Saturn. Most of the moons around Saturn was not known at the time the probe visited the system.

In 1990, Pioneer 11 travelled past the orbit of Pluto, and last contact with the probe was in November 1995.

Its estimated distance is about 17,28 billion km from Earth or about 115,5 AU as of September 2025. It will pass the star Lambda Aquilae in about 4 million years.

Pioneer 10

Pioneer 10 was launched on March 3rd, 1972. Pioneer 10 was also launched with an Atlas-Centaur rocket. It was the first probe to travel beyond the asteroid belt and take pictures of Jupiter up close.

It reached Jupiter in November 1973 and took about 500 pictures of the gas giant. While there it was discovered that the radiation from the planet was 10 times higher than previously predicted, and it was feared that the probe wouldn't survive the encounter. However, the probe made it through, only loosing a few of the images it had taken.

The last, very weak, signal was received on January 23rd, 2003.
Both Pioneer 10 and Pioneer 11 each carried a gold-anodized aluminum plaque depicting figures of a male and a female human, and also symbols showing the origin of the probe, should any intelligent alien species ever discover any of the probes.

Pioneer 10 is expected to pass nearby the star Aldebaran in about 2 million years.
As of July 2025, it is about 20.9 billion km from Earth, or about 139,7 AU.

Voyager 2

Launched August 20th 1977, 16 days before Voyager 1 on a Titan III-Centaur rocket, Voyager 2 is the 2nd furthest probe to leave Earth.

Its mission was to explore the outer planets and their moons; Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune. After visiting the planets, it was also going to measure the suns heliosphere and find the boundary between out solar system and interstellar space.

After visiting Jupiter and Saturn (1979 and 1981), Voyager 2 set it course towards Uranus. Reaching Uranus in 1985 it was the first (and only) probe to reach the first icy gas giant. It collected data and images from 5 of its 29 known moons before its closest approach to Uranus

In 1989 after nearly 12 years of flight, Voyager 2 reached Neptune, the farthest known planet in our Solar system. While there it observed the planet and 4 of the 16 known moons, before heading into interstellar space.

More than 40 years after being launched from Earth, it crossed the heliopause and was officially in interstellar space. In July 2023, Voyager 2 overtook Pioneer 10 as the second farthest spacecraft from the sun. In approximatly 2036, the probe will be out of range of the Deep Space Network, but is expected to not be able to power any of the instruments long before that.

Voyager 2 isn't travelling towards any specific star, and is expected to eternally travel the Milky Way. By now, it is about 21 billion km, or 140.5 AU from Earth.

Voyager 1

Voyager 1 was launched on September 5th, 1977 on the same type of rocket as Voyager 2. It is by far the farthest object launched from Earth, travelling at more that 1 AU (149.5 million km) per year further than Voyager 2.

Voyager 1 explored Jupiter and Saturn, the same as Voyager 2. However, instead of travelling to Uranus and Neptune, it took a detailed flyby of Titan, Saturns largest moon. It reached Titan in November 1980. The next day it also took pictures and collected data about Mimas, Enceladus, Rhea, and Hyperion; Four of Saturns many moons.

Voyager 1 overtook Voyager 2 already in December 1977, just 3 months after launch. It overtook Pioneer 10 in february 1998. In August 2012 it was confirmed to reach interstellar space.

Voyager 1 and 2 each brought with them the famous Voyager Golden Record. A disc with recorded audio of greetings in 55 different languages, several sounds of humans and animals, and also various music from different parts of the world. Several pictures, and even brainwave scans was also stored on the disc. On the surface of the disc, there was provided instructions for how to read the disc and also a map of how to find our Solar system.

As of now, Voyager 1 has travelled a stunning 25.2 billion km, or 168.35 AU from Earth. In November 2026 it is expected to reach a distance of 1 light day (24 light hours) from Earth. 1 light year will take about 18.000 years.
