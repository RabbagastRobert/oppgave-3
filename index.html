New Horizons

Launched January 19th, 2006, New Horizons is the 5th most distant object launched from Earth. It's primary mission was to study Pluto and at least one more Kuiper belt object.

New Horizons was launched on an Atlas V rocket, and at the time it was the fastest object launched from Earth with a speed measuring 58.500 km/h. (a record that has been vastly beaten by the Parker Solar Probe in 2023)

After a flyby and a gravity assist by Jupiter in 2007, New Horizons set the course for Pluto where it arrived in 2015. By then it was travelling at a stunning speed of 84.000 km/h at a distance of 12.500 km. It took the most detailed images of Pluto to this day.

The probe is now flying through the Kuiper belt, and is expected to exit the belt in 2028 / 2029. Unfortunatly, New Horizons, is planned to be shut down before then because of budget cuts at NASA.

Pioneer 11

Launched on an Atlas-Centaur rocket on april 6th, 1973, Pioneer 11 is the 4th most distant object to leave the Solar system. Its primary mission was to study Jupiter and Saturn, and also the solar wind and cosmic rays.

Pioneer 11 started its flyby of Jupiter in december 1975 where it took detailed images of Jupiters Red Spot. It also gathered data and images of several of Jupiters moons; Callisto, Ganymede, Io, Europa and Amalthea.

In late august 1979 it arrived at the Saturnian system. While there, it took pictures of Saturns rings while actually travelling through the rings. This was done to ensure that Voyager 2 could travel the same route through the rings without taking damage. Pioneer 11 was a true pioneer in that sense. This was done because the scientists thought it would be better to know ahead of time if it actually could be done. By doing this, two more moons was discovered sharing the same orbit. The probe nearly collided with one of them, travelling at a distance of only 4.000 km at the closest approach. Later, Pioneer 11 flew by Janus at a distance of only 2.500 km, however this was a planned encounter.

Pioneer 11 had flybys of 13 out of the 274 moons of Saturn. Most of the moons around Saturn was not known at the time the probe visited the system.

In 1990, Pioneer 11 travelled past the orbit of Pluto, and last contact with the probe was in November 1995.

Its estimated distance is about 17,28 billion km from Earth or about 115,5 AU as of September 2025. It will pass the star Lambda Aquilae in about 4 million years.

Pioneer 10

Pioneer 10 was launched on March 3rd, 1972. Pioneer 10 was also launched with an Atlas-Centaur rocket. It was the first probe to travel beyond the asteroid belt and take pictures of Jupiter up close.

It reached Jupiter in November 1973 and took about 500 pictures of the gas giant. While there it was discovered that the radiation from the planet was 10 times higher than previously predicted, and it was feared that the probe wouldn't survive the encounter. However, the probe made it through, only loosing a few of the images it had taken.

The last, very weak, signal was received on January 23rd, 2003.
Both Pioneer 10 and Pioneer 11 carried one of the famous gold-anodized aluminum plaque depicting figures of a male and a female human, and also symbols showing the origin of the probe, should any intelligent alien species ever discover any of the probes.

Pioneer 10 is expected to pass nearby the star Aldebaran in about 2 million years.
As of July 2025, it is about 20.9 billion km from Earth, or about 139,7 AU.
